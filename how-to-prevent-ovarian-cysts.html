How to Prevent Ovarian Cysts Naturally

Many women develop ovarian cysts every single cycle without knowing it. Most resolve on their own — but some don't, and the underlying hormonal imbalances that cause them can be addressed. Here is what you need to know about the types of cysts, why they form, when they become dangerous, and the full natural protocol for preventing them.

Ovarian cysts are one of those topics that tend to cause a lot of alarm — and understandably so. Finding out you have a cyst on your ovary can feel frightening, especially when you are not sure what it means or how serious it is. But here is something important to understand: a large proportion of ovarian cysts are functional, meaning they are part of your body's normal ovulatory process and will resolve on their own within a few weeks to a couple of months.

That does not mean you should dismiss them. Some cysts do become problematic, and there are other types of cysts that require medical management. The goal of this article is to give you a clear picture of what you are dealing with, what the warning signs are, and — most importantly — how to address the hormonal root causes so that cysts are less likely to form or grow in the first place.

Types of Ovarian Cysts

Not all ovarian cysts are the same. Understanding the type you have — or the type you want to prevent — changes how you approach it.

Functional Cysts

Functional cysts are by far the most common type. They arise directly from the ovulation process and are fluid-filled.

Follicular cysts form when a follicle — the fluid-filled sac that houses your egg — does not rupture and release the egg as it should. Instead of breaking open at ovulation, it continues to grow. Follicular cysts are almost always benign and typically resolve on their own within one to two menstrual cycles.

Corpus luteum cysts (also called luteal cysts) form after a follicle does release the egg. Normally, the empty follicle collapses and forms the corpus luteum, a temporary gland that produces progesterone. When the corpus luteum does not break down as it should and instead fills with fluid, a luteal cyst forms. Like follicular cysts, these are usually benign and self-resolving — but they can occasionally grow large enough to cause pain or rupture.

Pathological Cysts

Pathological cysts are structurally different from functional cysts. They tend to have both solid and fluid components, are not part of the normal ovulatory cycle, and typically require medical evaluation and often treatment.

  • Endometrioma — This type of cyst develops in women with endometriosis. It forms when endometrial-like tissue grows inside the ovary and fills with old blood, giving it a dark chocolate-like appearance (which is why it is often called a "chocolate cyst"). Endometriomas can significantly impair ovarian function and fertility, and they require management with an endometriosis specialist.
  • Cystadenoma — A cyst that develops from ovarian tissue and contains mucus or watery fluid. Cystadenomas are usually benign but can grow to large sizes if untreated. In rare cases they can be a precursor to ovarian cancer.
  • Dermoid cyst — An unusual type of cyst that can contain a range of tissues including hair, skin, bone, and teeth, because it forms from embryonic cells that can differentiate into many tissue types. Dermoid cysts are almost always benign but can grow large and sometimes require surgical removal.
  • PCOS-associated polycystic ovaries — In PCOS, the ovaries often contain multiple small follicles that never fully develop or release an egg. These are not true cysts in the traditional sense — they are immature follicles that accumulate because of disrupted ovulation — but they are part of the characteristic polycystic ovarian appearance on ultrasound.

This article's natural protocol is focused on functional cysts and PCOS-associated cyst formation, where hormonal intervention can make a meaningful difference. Pathological cysts like endometriomas, cystadenomas, and dermoid cysts require working closely with a medical doctor or specialist.

Why Ovarian Cysts Form: The Hormonal Root Causes

Functional ovarian cysts are fundamentally a problem of disrupted ovulation. In the normal cycle, one follicle matures, bursts open to release an egg, and then becomes the corpus luteum. When this process is disrupted — when the follicle either fails to rupture or the corpus luteum fails to break down properly — a cyst forms.

What disrupts the ovulatory process? Almost always, it comes down to hormonal imbalances upstream:

  • Dysregulated LH and FSH — These pituitary hormones direct the ovulatory process. When their signaling is off (as is common in PCOS, chronic stress, or thyroid dysfunction), the follicle may not receive the right signal to rupture.
  • Estrogen dominance — When estrogen levels are elevated relative to progesterone — whether due to excess production, impaired liver detoxification, or sluggish estrogen clearance through the gut — it creates an environment that promotes cyst formation and growth.
  • Low progesterone — Progesterone is produced after ovulation. If ovulation does not happen properly, or if the corpus luteum is weak, progesterone is low — which perpetuates the hormonal imbalance that drives further cyst formation.
  • Insulin resistance and blood sugar dysregulation — Elevated insulin drives up testosterone and disrupts LH/FSH signaling. This is a central mechanism in PCOS-related cyst formation, but it contributes to general ovulatory disruption in any woman.
  • Cortisol excess from chronic stress — The adrenal glands prioritize cortisol production under stress. Cortisol competes with progesterone and disrupts the HPO (hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian) axis that governs ovulation.
  • Thyroid dysfunction — Both hypothyroidism and elevated TSH impair follicle maturation and ovulatory signaling.

The key insight here is that ovarian cysts are not a primary disorder of the ovary — they are a downstream consequence of systemic hormonal imbalance. This is why addressing the root causes, rather than simply monitoring or suppressing with birth control, can make such a meaningful difference.

Symptoms of Ovarian Cysts

Many functional cysts produce no symptoms at all and are only discovered incidentally on an ultrasound. When symptoms are present, the most common include:

  • Pelvic pain, particularly around ovulation — and especially pain that is progressively worsening cycle over cycle
  • Lower abdominal cramping or pressure
  • Bloating or abdominal distension, particularly around mid-cycle
  • Irregular cycles or unpredictable periods
  • Absence of ovulation or anovulatory cycles
  • Spotting or light bleeding mid-cycle (beyond the 1–2 days of normal ovulation spotting)
  • Fertility challenges
  • In some cases, pain with urination or bowel movements if a cyst is pressing on adjacent structures

Red Flag Symptoms — Seek Medical Attention Immediately

If a cyst ruptures or ovarian torsion occurs (where the ovary twists and cuts off its blood supply), symptoms escalate rapidly. Ovarian cysts are the biggest risk factor for ovarian torsion. Call emergency services or go to an emergency room immediately if you experience:

  • Sudden, sharp, severe abdominal or pelvic pain
  • Pain accompanied by fever
  • Nausea and vomiting alongside pelvic pain
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, or feeling faint
  • Pain that is rapidly getting worse and is not easing

Both ovarian rupture and torsion are medical emergencies. Do not wait to see if these symptoms resolve on their own.

The Ovarian Cysts Protocol

The natural approach to preventing ovarian cysts works on several interconnected fronts: restoring healthy ovulation, correcting estrogen dominance, supporting progesterone production, reducing inflammation, and clearing the hormonal load from the liver. Here is the full protocol.

Step 1: Track Your Cycle and Confirm Ovulation

Everything starts here. If you are not ovulating consistently, your body cannot produce adequate progesterone — and without healthy progesterone, the hormonal conditions for cyst formation persist. Use Basal Body Temperature (BBT) charting and cervical fluid monitoring to confirm whether you are ovulating each cycle, and when. This is also how you will track whether the protocol is working.

Step 2: Address Estrogen Dominance

Estrogen dominance is one of the most consistently implicated hormonal factors in ovarian cyst formation. Here is how to address it through diet and targeted supplements:

Cruciferous vegetables daily — Broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, cabbage, kale, and arugula contain compounds called indole-3-carbinol (I3C) and diindylmethane (DIM) that support liver metabolism of estrogen through safer pathways (2-hydroxy estrogen rather than 16-hydroxy estrogen). Aim for 1–2 servings daily. Lightly cooking rather than eating fully raw can improve digestibility for some people.

Dietary fiber (25–35g daily) — Fiber binds to metabolized estrogen in the gut and carries it out of the body in stool. Without adequate fiber, metabolized estrogen is reabsorbed through the gut wall and recirculates. Beans, lentils, whole grains, seeds, and vegetables are your best sources.

DIM and I3C supplements — If dietary intake of cruciferous vegetables is limited, or if estrogen dominance is significant, supplementing with DIM (diindylmethane) or I3C (indole-3-carbinol) provides direct support for healthy estrogen metabolism. Follow label dosing. Start low — DIM in particular can cause a temporary detox response in some people.

Step 3: Support Progesterone Production

Progesterone counterbalances estrogen and is essential for resolving — not just preventing — functional cysts. The corpus luteum cyst, in particular, is directly linked to corpus luteum dysfunction and poor progesterone output.

  • Confirm you are ovulating — progesterone is almost entirely produced after ovulation, so healthy ovulation is the foundation of healthy progesterone levels.
  • Zinc (15–30 mg daily) — Zinc is required for follicle development, the ovulatory trigger, and corpus luteum function. It is a key cofactor in progesterone synthesis and is commonly depleted by hormonal birth control.
  • Vitamin B6 (25–50 mg daily as part of a B-complex) — B6 supports progesterone synthesis and helps lower elevated prolactin, which can suppress ovulation. Use a B-complex rather than isolated B6, as B vitamins work synergistically.
  • Vitex (chasteberry) — Vitex works by raising dopamine levels, which lowers prolactin and supports the LH output needed for healthy ovulation and corpus luteum function. If you have been on the foundational protocol for 3–4 months without adequate improvement, vitex is worth adding. Take 500 mg in capsule form in the morning from day 5 of your cycle through the first day of your next period, for a minimum of 3–6 months. Vitex is a potent herb — use under practitioner guidance when possible.

Step 4: Castor Oil Packs

Castor oil packs applied over the lower abdomen are one of the most effective and underused practices for ovarian cyst support. They work through several mechanisms:

  • Lymphatic stimulation — Castor oil has been shown to increase T-11 lymphocytes, boosting lymphatic flow and helping clear cellular waste, toxins, and inflammatory compounds from the pelvic region. Congestion in the lymphatic system around the ovaries can contribute to cyst persistence.
  • Improved pelvic circulation — The oil increases blood flow to the reproductive organs, delivering fresh, oxygenated, nutrient-rich blood to the ovaries and uterus. Better circulation supports follicle health, ovulation, and tissue repair.
  • Liver detoxification support — When used over the liver area (right side, below the rib cage), castor oil stimulates liver detoxification pathways that are essential for processing and clearing estrogen from the body.
  • Scar tissue and inflammation reduction — Castor oil's ricinoleic acid has anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. Consistent use can help reduce inflammation around existing cysts and break down scar tissue from previously ruptured cysts.

How to use: Apply 1–2 tablespoons of cold-pressed organic castor oil to the lower abdomen (below the navel) or over the liver. Cover with a flannel cloth and apply gentle heat via a hot water bottle. Leave on for 30–60 minutes. Alternatively, use a castor oil pack kit (such as the Queen of the Thrones pack) which can be worn for an hour or overnight. Use 3–4 times per week for at least one month. Alternate between pelvic placement and liver placement for best results.

Important: Do not use castor oil packs during your period, during pregnancy, or after ovulation if you are actively trying to conceive.

Step 5: Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition

Inflammation is both a trigger and a sustaining factor for ovarian cyst formation. The ovaries are highly sensitive to inflammatory signals, and chronic low-grade inflammation can impair ovulatory function. Dietary adjustments that reduce the inflammatory load make a meaningful difference:

  • Omega-3 fatty acids — Found in fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), walnuts, and flaxseed. Omega-3s reduce prostaglandin-mediated inflammation, support follicle health, and improve insulin sensitivity. A high-quality fish oil supplement (2–3 g EPA+DHA daily) is worth adding if dietary intake is low.
  • Reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugar — Refined carbs and sugar spike insulin, increase inflammation, and in PCOS can directly drive the LH/testosterone dysregulation that creates cysts. Prioritize complex carbohydrates, adequate protein, and healthy fats at every meal.
  • Eliminate dairy if sensitive — For some women, particularly those with PCOS or endometriomas, dairy increases inflammation and hormonal disruption. A 4–6 week dairy-free trial is a reasonable experiment to see if symptoms improve.
  • Adequate dietary fat — Cholesterol is the precursor to all steroid sex hormones including estrogen, progesterone, and testosterone. Very low-fat diets can impair ovarian hormone production. Do not fear healthy fats from eggs, avocado, olive oil, nuts, and quality animal products.

Step 6: Liver Support for Estrogen Clearance

The liver is the primary organ responsible for metabolizing and clearing used estrogen. When liver function is impaired — from poor diet, alcohol, toxin exposure, or nutritional deficiency — estrogen is not cleared efficiently and recirculates in the body, contributing to estrogen dominance and cyst formation.

  • Minimize alcohol, which directly impairs liver estrogen metabolism
  • Eat cruciferous vegetables daily (as outlined above)
  • Support bowel regularity (daily bowel movements are important — this is how metabolized estrogen leaves the body)
  • Consider milk thistle (silymarin) to support liver cell regeneration and detoxification capacity
  • Reduce exposure to xenoestrogens (synthetic estrogen-like compounds) in plastic containers, conventionally sprayed produce, and synthetic fragrance products

Step 7: Key Supplements for Ovarian Health

The following supplements have the most evidence and practical utility for ovarian cyst prevention and ovarian health. Consult your healthcare provider before starting new supplements, especially if you are taking medications.

NAC — N-acetyl cysteine (600 mg, two to three times daily) — NAC is one of the most well-studied natural supplements specifically for ovarian cysts. A 2013 randomized controlled trial published in the European Journal of Obstetrics & Gynecology found that NAC was significantly more effective than placebo at reducing the size of endometriomas, and subsequent research has supported its usefulness for PCOS-related cysts as well. NAC is a powerful antioxidant precursor that reduces oxidative stress in ovarian tissue, supports glutathione production (the body's master antioxidant), and improves insulin sensitivity. This is a particularly strong option for both functional cysts and PCOS.

Myo-inositol (2–6 g daily, divided into 2–3 doses) — Inositol is especially valuable for women with PCOS, who tend to have lower inositol levels in blood and tissues. Supplementing with myo-inositol improves insulin sensitivity, lowers elevated testosterone, and supports healthy ovulation — all of which reduce cyst formation in PCOS. Even without a PCOS diagnosis, myo-inositol supports general ovarian function and follicle health. Start with 1 g twice daily and increase gradually to avoid digestive upset. Some research supports combining myo-inositol with D-chiro-inositol in a 40:1 ratio for PCOS specifically.

Vitamin D (2,000–5,000 IU daily, or as guided by blood levels) — Vitamin D deficiency is extremely common and strongly associated with both PCOS and ovarian cyst formation. Vitamin D receptors are present throughout ovarian tissue, and adequate vitamin D is required for healthy follicle maturation and ovulatory function. Get your 25-OH vitamin D levels tested so you can supplement appropriately to reach optimal levels (60–80 ng/mL).

Magnesium glycinate (200–400 mg daily) — Magnesium is an essential cofactor in hundreds of biochemical reactions, including mitochondrial function in the ovaries. It has anti-inflammatory effects, supports progesterone production, improves insulin sensitivity, and reduces the muscle spasm-driven pain that can accompany cysts. Magnesium glycinate is gentle on the stomach and well absorbed.

Vitamin C (500–1,000 mg daily) — Vitamin C is highly concentrated in the corpus luteum and supports its function. It has been shown to improve progesterone production and protect follicles and ovarian tissue from oxidative stress. Use a whole-food vitamin C or ascorbic acid form, split into two doses.

Vitamin E (400–800 mg daily as mixed tocopherols and tocotrienols) — Vitamin E has been shown to improve ovarian function, increase follicle health, and enhance blood flow to the ovaries. It reduces oxidative stress in ovarian tissue, which helps support both healthy follicle development and corpus luteum function. Look for a supplement containing mixed tocopherols and tocotrienols, not just alpha-tocopherol alone.

Proteolytic (systemic) enzymes — As ovulation approaches, the body naturally increases its production of proteolytic enzymes to help break down the follicle wall so the egg can be released. Supplementing with a systemic enzyme blend (containing nattokinase, serrapeptase, bromelain, papain, and proteases) can support this process, reduce inflammation, improve circulation, and help break down scar tissue from previously ruptured cysts. Follow label dosing. Take on an empty stomach for systemic effect.

Recommended supplement sequencing: Start with myo-inositol and magnesium. After 4–6 weeks, add NAC and vitamin D. After another 4–6 weeks, if symptoms are persisting, add vitamin C, vitamin E, and proteolytic enzymes. Introduce supplements gradually so you can identify what is working.

Step 8: Blood Sugar and Stress

Two foundational drivers of ovarian cyst formation — insulin dysregulation and chronic stress — are often addressed last but should be treated as urgent priorities. Stabilizing blood sugar through adequate protein at every meal, reducing ultra-processed foods, and balancing carbohydrates with fat and fiber addresses the insulin-driven pathway. Managing chronic stress through sleep prioritization, nervous system support, and realistic stress reduction addresses the cortisol-driven pathway. Neither can be supplemented around.

When to See a Doctor

Natural approaches are powerful, but they are not a replacement for medical care in every situation. You should seek medical evaluation if:

  • You experience any of the red flag symptoms listed above (sudden severe pain, fever, nausea with pain, dizziness)
  • You have been diagnosed with a cyst larger than 5–6 cm that is not resolving over two to three menstrual cycles
  • Your pain is progressively worsening cycle over cycle
  • You are experiencing fertility challenges alongside recurrent cysts
  • An ultrasound shows a complex (solid + fluid) cyst, which warrants monitoring or investigation for pathological types
  • You suspect you have endometriosis — endometriomas require specialist management

Natural approaches work best alongside, not instead of, appropriate medical monitoring. A pelvic ultrasound gives you essential information about cyst type, size, and whether it is resolving. Use that information to guide how aggressively you implement the protocol above.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can ovarian cysts go away on their own without treatment?

Functional ovarian cysts — follicular cysts and corpus luteum cysts — very often do resolve on their own within one to three menstrual cycles. The body naturally reabsorbs the fluid as hormonal levels shift. This is why many doctors initially recommend watchful waiting with a follow-up ultrasound. That said, "going away on its own" does not address the hormonal root causes that allowed the cyst to form in the first place, which is why recurrence is common without deeper intervention.

What is the difference between a follicular cyst and a corpus luteum cyst?

Both are functional cysts that arise from the ovulation process. A follicular cyst forms when the follicle housing the egg fails to rupture and release the egg — it just keeps growing. A corpus luteum cyst forms after the egg has been released: the empty follicle, which normally becomes the corpus luteum (progesterone-producing gland), instead fills with fluid. Corpus luteum cysts are more associated with progesterone disruption and luteal phase irregularities, while follicular cysts are more associated with ovulatory disruption upstream.

Does PCOS cause ovarian cysts?

The "cysts" in polycystic ovarian syndrome are actually immature follicles that never fully developed or released an egg — not true cysts in the classic sense. They accumulate because ovulation is disrupted by the LH/FSH imbalances and elevated androgens characteristic of PCOS. A PCOS ultrasound typically shows 12 or more of these small follicles arranged around the ovary. The natural protocol for PCOS-associated ovarian changes focuses heavily on insulin sensitivity (myo-inositol, blood sugar stabilization), testosterone normalization, and restoring consistent ovulation.

Is ovarian pain during ovulation a sign of a cyst?

Not necessarily — some mid-cycle pain (known as mittelschmerz) is a normal part of ovulation for many women. However, pain that is significantly worsening each cycle, that is sharp and severe rather than mild and fleeting, or that lasts more than a day or two warrants an ultrasound to rule out a cyst. Increasing ovulation pain is one of the symptoms the protocol specifically aims to address, and it is one of the reasons tracking your cycle carefully is so important.

Why do I keep getting ovarian cysts even when they resolve each time?

Recurrent ovarian cysts are a sign that the underlying hormonal environment keeps recreating the conditions for cyst formation. Common culprits include ongoing estrogen dominance (often from impaired liver detoxification or poor estrogen clearance through the gut), persistent ovulatory disruption from stress or blood sugar issues, or PCOS. This is why the natural protocol addresses root causes — not just the individual cyst — and why supplements alone are not enough without the dietary and lifestyle foundations.

Can NAC really help shrink ovarian cysts?

There is genuine research support for NAC and ovarian cysts. A randomized controlled trial found NAC significantly more effective than placebo at reducing endometrioma size over three months. For PCOS specifically, NAC has been shown to improve ovulation rates, reduce testosterone, and improve insulin sensitivity — all of which reduce the follicular accumulation characteristic of PCOS. It is one of the most evidence-backed natural supplements for ovarian cyst support, particularly at doses of 600 mg two to three times daily. It is not a substitute for medical management of large or complex cysts, but it is a well-grounded addition to a natural protocol.

Are castor oil packs safe to use if I have an ovarian cyst right now?

Castor oil packs applied to the lower abdomen are generally considered safe for functional ovarian cysts and are a traditional supportive practice for reducing cyst size and relieving pelvic congestion. Do not use them during your period, during pregnancy, or after ovulation if you are actively trying to conceive. If your cyst is large, complex, or if you are experiencing significant pain, check with your healthcare provider before starting any new practice. During a pain flare, applying heat alone (without the castor oil pack) can help manage discomfort while you seek evaluation.

Will the birth control pill prevent ovarian cysts from coming back?

The birth control pill is sometimes prescribed to prevent new functional cysts by suppressing ovulation — no ovulation, in theory, means no follicular or corpus luteum cysts. However, the pill does not address existing cysts, and some women continue to develop cysts even while on hormonal contraception. More importantly, the pill does not address the hormonal root causes that make you prone to cysts. When you stop taking it, the underlying imbalances remain — or may even be compounded by the post-pill period of hormonal re-regulation. This is why a root-cause approach to ovarian health is ultimately more effective for long-term prevention.
