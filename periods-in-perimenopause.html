Periods in Perimenopause: What Is Actually Happening and What You Can Do About It

Perimenopause can begin in your late 30s or early 40s and last up to a decade. Your periods may get heavier, more erratic, and wildly unpredictable — and it is not random. Here is the full hormonal picture, plus a natural protocol for navigating this transition well.

I have heard every version of this story: a woman in her early 40s who has had relatively normal periods her whole life suddenly starts flooding through her clothes, having cycles that show up after 22 days, then vanishing for three months. Or a woman in her late 30s who notices her periods have started arriving a few days earlier than usual, her PMS has become volcanic, and she is waking up at 3 a.m. drenched in sweat. Her doctor runs a few tests, tells her everything looks "normal," and offers the pill.

What is happening in these scenarios — and in so many more like them — is perimenopause. And the reason so many women are blindsided by it is that we are rarely told the truth about what this transition actually looks like, when it starts, and why the symptoms are happening in the first place.

This article is the thorough explanation I wish every woman in her late 30s and 40s received. It covers what perimenopause is, what it does to your periods and hormones, how to tell if you are in it, and a comprehensive natural protocol — including when to consider bioidentical hormone therapy — for navigating it without suffering your way through it.

What Is Perimenopause, and When Does It Start?

Perimenopause is the transitional phase leading up to menopause, during which your ovaries gradually shift from regular, predictable hormone production toward the eventual cessation of menstrual cycles. The word literally means "around menopause" — and it encompasses the often dramatic hormonal fluctuations that precede your final period.

Here is what most women are not told: perimenopause can begin as early as the mid-to-late 30s, well before most people imagine it could be relevant. The average age of onset is the early-to-mid 40s, but the hormonal changes that kick it off — most notably the gradual decline in ovulation quality and consistency — begin in the mid-30s for many women. The average duration of perimenopause is 4 to 10 years before the final menstrual period. Menopause itself (the official day after 12 consecutive months without a period) occurs on average around age 51.

This means a woman can spend a significant portion of her 40s in perimenopause, experiencing symptoms that range from subtle (slightly shorter cycles, heightened PMS) to severe (flooding periods, insomnia, mood swings, heart palpitations) — and often not get an explanation that makes sense.

Perimenopause is a clinical diagnosis. There is no single blood test that confirms it. FSH, AMH, and estradiol levels fluctuate so widely during this transition that a single measurement is often misleading. If you are in your late 30s or 40s and experiencing the symptoms described in this article, that pattern itself is meaningful diagnostic information — even if labs come back "normal."

How Your Periods Change in Perimenopause

Period changes are usually the first noticeable sign that perimenopause has begun. The pattern tends to follow a predictable arc, though the timing varies considerably from woman to woman. I think of perimenopause as unfolding in two broad phases:

Phase 1: High Estrogen, Low Progesterone

  • Shorter cycles (less than 25 days)
  • Heavier, longer periods
  • More intense PMS
  • Anovulatory cycles become more frequent
  • Spotting mid-cycle or between periods
  • Cycles start to vary — some short, some normal, some skipped

Phase 2: Dropping Estrogen and Progesterone

  • Longer cycles, fewer periods per year
  • Periods becoming lighter and shorter
  • Hot flashes and night sweats intensify
  • Longer gaps between periods (months at a time)
  • Irregular breakthrough bleeding
  • Eventually, periods stop entirely

Why Cycles Get Shorter First

In the earliest stage of perimenopause, one of the first changes women notice is that their cycles become shorter — arriving every 22 or 23 days instead of every 28. This happens because the follicular phase (the first half of the cycle, when an egg develops and estrogen rises) shortens. As the ovaries age, the pituitary gland releases higher and higher levels of follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) in an effort to recruit follicles and prompt ovulation. The ovary responds, but ovulation happens earlier than it used to — resulting in a compressed cycle.

Then Cycles Become Wildly Variable

As perimenopause progresses, the regularity that characterized your earlier cycles tends to break down. You might have a 24-day cycle, then a 35-day cycle, then skip a month entirely. Anovulatory cycles — cycles where no egg is released — become increasingly common. Without ovulation, the corpus luteum (the temporary structure that forms after ovulation and produces progesterone) never forms. The result: estrogen continues building the uterine lining without progesterone to stabilize it, creating the classic estrogen-dominant state.

Why Periods Get Heavier

This is one of the most distressing changes for many women, and it has a clear hormonal explanation. When anovulatory cycles increase, progesterone production drops. Without progesterone to stabilize the uterine lining, estrogen continues to build it up. When that lining eventually sheds, the bleeding is heavier, longer, and sometimes accompanied by large clots. This is directly linked to the estrogen dominance pattern of early perimenopause — a topic I cover in much more depth in the article on heavy periods.

Eventually, Periods Taper Off

In later perimenopause, as estradiol (the predominant estrogen of the cycling years) begins to decline alongside progesterone, the pendulum swings the other way: periods become lighter, shorter, and less frequent. Cycles stretch to every six to eight weeks, then longer. There may be occasional spotting or breakthrough bleeding as the ovaries wind down their final functions. Eventually, periods stop entirely — and after 12 consecutive months without one, you have officially reached menopause.

The Hormonal Story: What Is Really Happening

The period changes above are not random — they are the visible expression of deep hormonal shifts. Understanding what is happening with each hormone makes the whole picture coherent.

FSH: The First Signal Something Is Shifting

FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) is released by the pituitary gland to stimulate follicle development in the ovary. As the ovaries age and their follicle reserve diminishes, the pituitary has to work harder to get a response — releasing more and more FSH. Elevated FSH is often the first measurable hormonal marker of perimenopause. However, because FSH fluctuates so dramatically throughout this transition, a single elevated reading is not conclusive on its own.

Estrogen: High, Then Erratic, Then Declining

Here is the part that surprises most women: in early perimenopause, estrogen does not simply decline — it often spikes to higher levels than you have ever experienced. Just as a teenager's ovaries fluctuate wildly as they establish their rhythm, the aging ovaries fluctuate as they figure out how to wind down. The pituitary's increasing FSH output can drive bursts of estrogen production that far exceed what was normal in your 20s and 30s. This is the estrogen dominance window — and it can coexist with the symptoms of high estrogen (heavy periods, breast tenderness, raging PMS, migraines) and low estrogen (hot flashes, night sweats, vaginal dryness) simultaneously, sometimes even within the same cycle.

Estrogen dominance means estrogen is too high relative to progesterone — and this can happen even when estrogen levels are not objectively elevated, as long as progesterone is insufficient to balance them.

Progesterone: The Hormone That Takes the Biggest Hit

Progesterone is entirely dependent on ovulation. A healthy corpus luteum — the structure that forms after an egg is released — is required to produce meaningful amounts of progesterone. As anovulatory cycles increase in perimenopause, progesterone production becomes sporadic and eventually minimal. This decline has cascading effects throughout the body, because progesterone does far more than regulate the second half of the menstrual cycle. It supports sleep, calms the nervous system, protects brain function, supports bone density, modulates thyroid activity, and helps regulate mood. Its loss is felt everywhere. For more on why this matters and what to do about it, read my article on low progesterone.

Testosterone: A More Gradual Decline — With Some Surprises

Testosterone declines more gradually than estrogen and progesterone during perimenopause, but it does decline — and with it go libido, stamina, muscle tone, and energy. However, there is a wrinkle: testosterone can temporarily rise during perimenopause due to erratic FSH surges stimulating androgen production from the ovaries and declining levels of sex hormone binding globulin (SHBG), which normally binds testosterone and keeps it inactive. This transient rise can cause new facial hair growth, acne, and hair thinning at the scalp — symptoms that baffle women who do not expect higher androgens in perimenopause.

Insulin resistance, which is very common in perimenopause due to the loss of estrogen and progesterone (which both have insulin-sensitizing effects), can also increase ovarian testosterone production and further lower SHBG. Addressing blood sugar is therefore doubly important during this transition.

The hormonal fluctuations of perimenopause do not limit themselves to your period. Because sex hormones influence virtually every system in the body, their erratic decline produces a wide-ranging constellation of symptoms.

This is not a short list — and it explains why perimenopause can feel like your body is falling apart from multiple directions at once. What ties it all together is the hormonal disruption underneath: erratic estrogen, declining progesterone, rising insulin resistance, and dysregulated adrenal function.

How to Know If You Are in Perimenopause

Perimenopause is primarily a clinical diagnosis — meaning it is diagnosed based on your age, your symptoms, and the pattern of your cycles, not a single blood test. This is important, because many women are told "your labs are normal" and sent home with no explanation for what they are experiencing.

The most useful tests in this context are:

  • FSH: An elevated FSH (above 10–12 IU/L on day 3 of the cycle) suggests the pituitary is working harder than usual to stimulate ovulation. However, FSH fluctuates significantly throughout the perimenopausal transition, so a single result tells only part of the story. An FSH above 40 IU/L on two measurements taken 4–6 weeks apart (in the absence of a period) suggests menopause is near or has occurred.
  • AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone): A marker of ovarian reserve — how many follicles remain. Declining AMH provides context for where you are in the transition, though it does not diagnose perimenopause on its own.
  • Estradiol: Highly variable throughout the perimenopausal transition. A single low reading does not confirm late perimenopause; a very high reading alongside symptoms is consistent with early perimenopause estrogen dominance.
  • Progesterone (day 21 or 7 days post-ovulation): A useful test to assess whether you are ovulating and producing adequate progesterone. A level below 5 ng/mL suggests inadequate ovulation or no ovulation in that cycle.
  • DUTCH test (Dried Urine Test for Comprehensive Hormones): Provides the most complete picture of hormone levels, metabolites, and detoxification pathways. Particularly useful if you are considering targeted supplementation or hormone therapy.

Do not be discouraged if your doctor tells you your labs are normal. Testing during perimenopause is genuinely difficult because hormone levels change so rapidly. Symptom patterns and cycle history matter as much — often more — than any single snapshot test.

The Natural Support Protocol for Perimenopause

Perimenopause does not have to be a decade of suffering. The hormonal fluctuations are real and significant, but the degree to which they disrupt daily life is strongly influenced by the foundational state of your health going into this transition. Here is the protocol I have used with thousands of women navigating this stage.

Foundation

Blood Sugar Stability — The Most Underrated Priority

Insulin resistance is one of the hallmarks of perimenopause. Estrogen and progesterone both have insulin-sensitizing effects on the body — meaning they help your cells respond appropriately to insulin. As these hormones decline, your insulin sensitivity declines with them. For many women, this is the first time in their lives they experience blood sugar dysregulation, and it makes everything else worse: hot flashes intensify, sleep deteriorates, weight shifts to the abdomen, and mood becomes volatile.

  • Build every meal around protein and fiber first — these blunt the blood sugar response to carbohydrates
  • Eat within 30–60 minutes of waking to prevent a cortisol-driven blood sugar spike in the morning
  • Limit refined carbohydrates and ultra-processed foods, particularly in the evening
  • Include 2–3 tablespoons of healthy fat (olive oil, avocado, nuts) at each meal to slow glucose absorption
  • Walk for 10–15 minutes after meals — post-meal movement is one of the single most effective tools for blood sugar management and is underused by almost everyone
  • Consider berberine (500 mg twice daily with meals) if blood sugar issues are significant — it has comparable effects to metformin in some studies and is available without a prescription
Step 1

Manage Estrogen Dominance: Liver and Gut Support

In early perimenopause, the dominant hormonal problem for most women is excess estrogen relative to declining progesterone. Supporting the body's ability to clear used estrogen is foundational. The liver processes and packages estrogen for excretion, and the gut excretes it. When either system is sluggish, estrogen recirculates and worsens the imbalance driving heavy periods, breast tenderness, and raging PMS.

Dietary strategies:

  • Eat cruciferous vegetables daily (broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower, kale) — they support the 2-OH estrogen metabolite pathway and shift estrogen metabolism toward a healthier balance
  • Add 1 tablespoon of ground flaxseed daily — its lignans support healthy estrogen metabolism and provide phytoestrogen activity that can help moderate estrogen fluctuations
  • Prioritize daily bowel movements — estrogen is excreted through the stool, and constipation allows it to be reabsorbed. High-fiber diet plus adequate hydration is essential.
  • Limit alcohol significantly — alcohol impairs liver estrogen metabolism more than almost any other common dietary factor

Targeted supplements:

  • DIM (diindolylmethane): a concentrated cruciferous-derived compound that supports phase 1 liver estrogen detoxification and helps lower excess estradiol. Appropriate when estrogen is confirmed high. Not ideal if estrogen is already low — this is why testing before using DIM is wise. Typical dose: 100–200 mg daily with food, for 3–4 cycles.
  • Magnesium glycinate: essential for phase 2 liver detoxification and hundreds of enzymatic reactions. 200–400 mg at night also supports sleep. One of the most useful supplements across all of perimenopause.
  • Milk thistle, NAC, and sulforaphane: a combination of liver-supportive compounds that enhance both phase 1 and phase 2 estrogen detoxification. Look for a comprehensive liver support formula containing two or more of these.
Step 2

Support Progesterone Production

Because declining ovulation is the core driver of progesterone loss in perimenopause, supporting the quality of the ovulation that does still occur matters. You cannot fully restore progesterone to premenopausal levels in perimenopause through natural means alone — but you can meaningfully support whatever progesterone production remains, and in some cases bioidentical progesterone supplementation is appropriate (more on this below).

  • Zinc: directly supports healthy corpus luteum function and progesterone production. 25–30 mg daily with food (take copper alongside it — typically 1–2 mg — if using zinc long-term, as zinc depletes copper).
  • Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine or pyridoxal-5-phosphate): supports progesterone production and reduces estrogen dominance symptoms, including PMS, mood changes, and breast tenderness. 50–100 mg daily as P5P (the active form).
  • Vitex (Chaste tree berry): works on the pituitary to support LH production, which stimulates progesterone after ovulation. Most useful in early perimenopause when ovulation is still occurring but inconsistently. Requires 3–6 months of consistent use to assess benefit. Not appropriate in late perimenopause or menopause when ovulation has ceased.
  • Blood sugar and stress management are also foundational here — both directly disrupt the HPO axis signaling required for ovulation. Read my article on how stress affects your menstrual cycle for more detail on this mechanism.
Step 3

Bone Health Support

Estrogen plays a central protective role in maintaining bone density. Its decline in perimenopause — and especially in the first several years after menopause — accelerates bone turnover and increases the risk of osteoporosis. Bone protection should begin now, not after menopause is confirmed.

  • Calcium: aim for 1,000–1,200 mg daily from food and supplements combined. Food sources (dairy, sardines with bones, leafy greens) are preferable to supplements, as high-dose supplemental calcium carries some cardiovascular risk when not paired with K2. If supplementing, calcium citrate is better absorbed than calcium carbonate, especially in women with low stomach acid.
  • Vitamin D3: necessary for calcium absorption and bone mineralization, as well as immune function, mood, and insulin sensitivity. Test your level (25-OH vitamin D) — most women need at least 2,000–5,000 IU daily to maintain optimal levels (70–100 ng/mL).
  • Vitamin K2 (MK-7 form): directs calcium into bones rather than into soft tissue and arteries. 100–200 mcg daily. Always take K2 alongside D3 — they work synergistically.
  • Magnesium: required for over 300 enzymatic reactions, including those involved in bone matrix formation. Most women are deficient. Magnesium glycinate or malate, 200–400 mg daily.
  • Weight-bearing exercise: the most powerful non-nutritional stimulus for bone density. Resistance training (2–3 times weekly) and high-impact activities (walking, jogging, dancing, tennis) are superior to swimming or cycling for bone health specifically.
Step 4

Sleep and Nervous System Support

Sleep disruption in perimenopause is multi-layered. Night sweats wake you up. Declining progesterone removes its sedating, anxiolytic effect on the nervous system. Cortisol dysregulation (increasingly common as adrenal function is asked to compensate for declining ovarian hormone production) disrupts the natural cortisol rhythm that governs sleep and waking. And blood sugar drops at night can trigger early-morning waking.

  • Magnesium glycinate at night (200–400 mg): one of the most consistently useful supplements for sleep in perimenopause. Supports GABA signaling, calms the nervous system, and reduces nighttime muscle tension.
  • Ashwagandha (300–600 mg of a standardized KSM-66 extract): an adaptogen that modulates the HPA axis, reduces cortisol reactivity, and has been shown in clinical trials to improve sleep quality and reduce anxiety in women during this transition.
  • Phosphatidylserine (100–200 mg before bed): supports healthy cortisol clearing in the evening, which is essential for falling and staying asleep.
  • Keep the bedroom cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C) and dark — temperature sensitivity heightens significantly in perimenopause, and even a slightly warm room can trigger night sweats.
  • Avoid screens 60 minutes before bed and limit alcohol, which dramatically worsens sleep architecture and triggers night sweats.
  • Prioritize adrenal health — read the section on stress below. The adrenal glands become the primary producers of certain hormones in menopause, so their functional state matters enormously.
Step 5

Phytoestrogens: Flaxseed and Soy

Phytoestrogens are plant compounds that interact weakly with estrogen receptors. In the context of perimenopause, they are particularly interesting because they can act as selective estrogen receptor modulators — binding to estrogen receptors and producing a mild estrogenic effect when estrogen is low (helping with hot flashes and vaginal dryness) while also competing with estrogen for receptor binding when estrogen is high (potentially reducing estrogen dominance symptoms).

Flaxseed: 1–2 tablespoons of freshly ground flaxseed daily is one of the most evidence-supported phytoestrogen strategies. Its lignans support healthy estrogen metabolism via the gut microbiome and have been shown to reduce hot flash frequency and severity. Grind fresh (pre-ground flax oxidizes quickly) and add to smoothies, oatmeal, or yogurt.

Soy: There is more controversy here, but the evidence from human clinical trials — including data from Japanese women who have consumed traditional soy throughout their lives — is generally supportive of fermented soy foods (miso, tempeh, natto) in moderate amounts for perimenopausal symptoms. The concern about soy and breast cancer risk applies primarily to concentrated soy protein supplements and isoflavone extracts, not whole or fermented soy foods. For most women without a history of estrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer, 1–2 servings of whole or fermented soy daily is a reasonable choice, not a risk. Discuss with your doctor if you have a personal or family history of hormone-sensitive cancer.

Step 6

Adrenal and Thyroid Support

Two systems that are often overlooked in perimenopause but profoundly affect the experience of it: the adrenal glands and the thyroid. The adrenal glands take over a portion of hormone production — including small amounts of progesterone and the precursors for estrogen — as the ovaries wind down. If you arrive at this transition already exhausted and cortisol-dysregulated, the adrenal glands are not equipped to handle this additional demand, and symptoms will be significantly worse.

The thyroid is also closely linked to both ovarian function and the symptoms of perimenopause. Hypothyroidism and perimenopause share many symptoms (fatigue, weight gain, brain fog, hair loss, irregular periods), and the two conditions commonly co-occur. A full thyroid panel — including TSH, free T3, free T4, reverse T3, TPO antibodies, and thyroglobulin antibodies — is essential if you have a cluster of these symptoms.

  • Prioritize adrenal recovery: reduce overcommitment, protect sleep, eat regularly to prevent blood sugar swings that spike cortisol, and consider adaptogenic support (ashwagandha, rhodiola, schisandra)
  • Test thyroid thoroughly — TSH alone is not sufficient
  • Reduce stimulant dependency (excessive caffeine drives cortisol and worsens adrenal burnout)

Bioidentical Hormone Replacement Therapy (BHRT): When to Consider It

Let me be direct about this: I think we should do whatever we need to do to feel well during this transition, and for some women, that includes hormone therapy. There is no virtue in suffering through severe perimenopause symptoms when effective options exist.

Bioidentical hormones are hormones that are chemically identical in structure to the hormones produced by your body — as opposed to synthetic hormones (like those in most traditional HRT formulations), which are structurally different and interact with hormone receptors differently. The two most commonly used bioidentical hormones in perimenopause are bioidentical progesterone (often as oral micronized progesterone, sold as Prometrium) and bioidentical estradiol (transdermal patch, gel, or cream).

Who Might Benefit From BHRT?

  • Women with severe, persistent hot flashes and night sweats that significantly impair sleep and daily function
  • Women with significant mood instability, anxiety, or depression that began with perimenopause and has not responded adequately to natural interventions
  • Women with very heavy or unpredictable bleeding that natural approaches have not adequately addressed after 3–4 committed cycles
  • Women with notable bone density loss on DEXA scan who are at risk for osteoporosis
  • Women with significant vaginal dryness and genitourinary symptoms (low-dose local vaginal estrogen is highly effective here and has minimal systemic absorption)
  • Women who have tried foundational diet and lifestyle interventions and continue to have a severely disrupted quality of life

The Evidence and Current Thinking

The fear of hormone replacement therapy was largely seeded by the 2002 Women's Health Initiative study, which found increased risks of breast cancer, blood clots, and cardiovascular events. What is important to understand is that the WHI used synthetic hormones — specifically conjugated equine estrogen (from horse urine) and medroxyprogesterone acetate (a synthetic progestin, not bioidentical progesterone). Subsequent research has painted a more nuanced picture:

  • Transdermal (patch or gel) estradiol does not carry the same blood clot risk as oral estrogen, because it bypasses first-pass liver metabolism
  • Bioidentical micronized progesterone appears to carry a more favorable cardiovascular and breast cancer risk profile compared to synthetic progestins
  • Hormone therapy initiated in early perimenopause or within 10 years of menopause may actually reduce cardiovascular risk — this is the "timing hypothesis" that has gained significant support in the research literature
  • The absolute risk increases associated with HRT, even in the original WHI study, were small — less than 1 additional case of breast cancer per 1,000 women per year

How to Approach BHRT With Your Doctor

BHRT requires working with a knowledgeable practitioner — ideally a physician with specific training in women's hormonal health, menopause, or functional medicine. Here is how to approach the conversation:

  • Request comprehensive hormone testing before starting — ideally a DUTCH test or, at minimum, serum FSH, estradiol, progesterone, and testosterone
  • Discuss the different delivery options: patches, gels, and creams carry lower cardiovascular risk than oral forms; vaginal formulations for genitourinary symptoms have minimal systemic absorption
  • Ask specifically about bioidentical progesterone (micronized, as Prometrium or a compounded equivalent) — not synthetic progestins
  • Establish a follow-up and monitoring plan — hormone levels should be retested 3–6 months after starting any BHRT, and ongoing monitoring of breast health and other relevant markers is standard
  • Understand that BHRT can be used short-term as a bridge while you address underlying factors, or long-term if symptoms return after discontinuation — both are valid approaches

Bioidentical hormones are not a replacement for the foundational work described earlier in this article. But for women who are doing the work and still struggling significantly, they are a powerful and often underutilized tool.

A Note on Hormonal Birth Control in Perimenopause

One of the most common things I see is women in perimenopause being offered — or remaining on — hormonal birth control as a "solution" to the period chaos and symptoms they are experiencing. This approach is deeply flawed. Hormonal birth control suppresses your remaining ovarian function, replaces your natural hormones with synthetic ones, and masks the symptoms without addressing what is driving them. It also makes it impossible to track your actual hormonal status during the transition.

This is not to say that hormonal birth control is never appropriate. For some women with genuinely unmanageable bleeding while they are working on root-cause interventions, a short-term bridge may be reasonable. But it is not a perimenopause strategy — it is a symptom suppressor. A whole-body approach, ideally supported by a practitioner who understands the full complexity of this transition, is what actually moves the needle.

Frequently Asked Questions About Periods in Perimenopause

How do I know if my irregular periods are perimenopause or something else?

Several things can cause irregular periods in your late 30s and 40s beyond perimenopause — thyroid dysfunction, elevated prolactin, significant stress, PCOS, or structural issues like fibroids or polyps. Before attributing irregular periods solely to perimenopause, it is worth ruling these out with basic testing: a full thyroid panel (not just TSH), prolactin, a pelvic ultrasound if periods have suddenly become heavy or erratic, and a comprehensive hormone assessment. That said, if you are in your 40s and your cycles have shifted in the patterns described in this article — shorter, then more variable, with worsening PMS and sleep disruption — perimenopause is a likely explanation, even if your labs look "normal."

Can perimenopause start in the late 30s? That seems too early.

Yes, it genuinely can. While the average onset of perimenopause is the early-to-mid 40s, the underlying hormonal shifts — particularly declining ovulation quality and early progesterone changes — can begin in the mid-to-late 30s. Women who have had multiple pregnancies, have a history of eating disorders, have smoked, or have a family history of early menopause may enter perimenopause earlier. Signs to watch for in your late 30s: cycles that have shortened (less than 26 days), heightened PMS that was not previously an issue, sleep disruption around your period, increased anxiety, and new onset of night sweats.

My periods are getting much heavier in perimenopause. Is this normal?

It is extremely common — but "common" does not mean you should just accept it. The mechanism is well understood: increasing anovulatory cycles mean declining progesterone, which allows estrogen to build the uterine lining without the usual stabilization and signaling for clean shedding. The result is heavier, longer, sometimes clottier bleeding. The same natural strategies that help heavy periods generally — estrogen dominance support, progesterone optimization, liver function, blood sugar stability — are the place to start. If bleeding is severe enough to cause significant anemia or is not responding to natural interventions, medical evaluation (including an ultrasound to rule out structural contributors) and potentially hormonal support are warranted.

My doctor says my FSH is normal, so I can't be in perimenopause. Is that right?

Not necessarily. FSH fluctuates enormously throughout the perimenopausal transition — it can be elevated in one cycle and normal in the next. A single normal FSH measurement does not rule out perimenopause. Similarly, AMH declines gradually and a "normal" AMH does not mean hormonal changes are not underway. Perimenopause is a clinical diagnosis: your age, your symptom pattern, and changes in your cycle length and character are the most meaningful indicators. If your symptoms strongly suggest perimenopause and your labs are normal, push for a clinical conversation about what you are experiencing — not just a reassurance based on a single data point.

I am 45 and have not had a period in four months. Am I in menopause?

Not yet — at least not officially. Menopause is defined as 12 consecutive months without a period. Periods can stop for several months during perimenopause and then return — and if they do, the 12-month clock resets. That said, extended gaps between periods do become increasingly common in later perimenopause. If you are in your 40s and have not had a period for several months and have other symptoms (hot flashes, night sweats, vaginal dryness), your body is likely in the later stages of the transition. A pregnancy test is always worth ruling out first, especially if you have had any unprotected sex.

What is the difference between bioidentical and synthetic hormones?

Bioidentical hormones are chemically identical to the hormones naturally produced by your body — they have the same molecular structure as your own estradiol, progesterone, and testosterone. Synthetic hormones (like conjugated equine estrogen or medroxyprogesterone acetate, used in traditional HRT) are structurally different from human hormones and interact with receptors differently. The distinction matters because the risks identified in the 2002 WHI study were associated specifically with synthetic hormones, not bioidentical ones. Current evidence suggests that bioidentical estradiol (delivered transdermally) and bioidentical micronized progesterone have more favorable risk profiles than their synthetic counterparts — though all hormone therapy should be used under medical supervision with appropriate monitoring.

Do I still need contraception during perimenopause?

Yes. As long as you are still getting periods — even irregularly — ovulation is possible, and pregnancy is possible. Spontaneous ovulation can occur even when cycles are erratic and FSH is elevated. The rate of unintended pregnancy in perimenopausal women is surprisingly higher than most people assume. Contraception should be maintained until you have had 12 consecutive months without a period if you are over 50, or 24 consecutive months if you are under 50. Non-hormonal options (copper IUD, condoms, barrier methods) preserve your ability to track your natural hormonal patterns without interference.

Will my symptoms get worse before they get better?

For many women, the most symptomatic period is the middle of the transition — once anovulatory cycles are common but before periods have fully stopped. Early perimenopause can feel quite destabilizing because of the high-estrogen, low-progesterone state that drives heavy bleeding, mood swings, and worsening PMS. Many women find that symptoms actually ease somewhat as they move into later perimenopause and estrogen levels begin to decline along with progesterone — though new low-estrogen symptoms (hot flashes, vaginal dryness) may emerge. The natural protocol in this article is designed to reduce symptom severity at every stage, and the earlier you begin supporting your hormonal health, the better positioned you will be throughout the transition.
