Irregular Periods: What They're Telling You About Your Hormones

An irregular cycle is one of the most common signs that something is off hormonally — but what counts as irregular, and what's actually driving it?

Irregular PeriodsPCOSThyroid

What Counts as Irregular?

A normal menstrual cycle falls between 21 and 35 days in length, measured from the first day of one period to the first day of the next. Within that range, some natural variation is expected — it is normal for your cycle length to shift by a few days from month to month. What is not normal, and deserves investigation, is consistent variation greater than seven to nine days, cycles that regularly fall outside the 21–35 day window, or a cycle pattern that has changed noticeably without an obvious explanation.

Irregularity can look different for different women. For some it means cycles that arrive every six to eight weeks instead of monthly. For others it means periods that seem unpredictable — sometimes 24 days, sometimes 40. Some women experience spotting between periods that blurs the picture entirely. All of these patterns are meaningful, and all of them reflect something happening in the hormonal chain that drives your cycle.

One important clarification: irregular cycles in the first one to two years after menstruation begins (menarche) and in the years approaching menopause (perimenopause) are expected due to the hormonal immaturity and decline at each end of the reproductive lifespan. Outside those windows, irregularity is a signal worth taking seriously.

Why Ovulation Is the Key

The menstrual cycle is, at its core, an ovulation cycle. Your period is the end result of an ovulation that occurred roughly two weeks prior. When ovulation is delayed, disrupted, or absent, the timing of your period is thrown off accordingly. This is why the most useful question when investigating irregular cycles is not "why is my period late?" but rather "why did I not ovulate on schedule — or at all?"

Ovulation is governed by a precise hormonal cascade: GnRH from the hypothalamus triggers FSH from the pituitary, which stimulates follicle development in the ovaries. As the dominant follicle matures it produces rising estrogen, which eventually triggers the LH surge that causes the follicle to release the egg. Any disruption to this cascade — from the hypothalamus, the pituitary, the ovaries themselves, or the broader hormonal environment — can delay or prevent ovulation.

A regular period does not guarantee ovulation. It is possible to bleed on a regular schedule while still not ovulating consistently — particularly in women approaching perimenopause or those with certain hormonal imbalances. Tracking basal body temperature or using ovulation predictor kits can help clarify whether ovulation is occurring.

Common Causes

Irregular cycles have many possible drivers, and more than one is often present at the same time. The most frequently seen causes in clinical practice include:

  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) — the most common hormonal disorder in women of reproductive age, affecting ovulation directly
  • Thyroid dysfunction — both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism can disrupt cycle regularity and ovulation timing
  • Chronic psychological or physical stress, which suppresses GnRH pulsatility and delays ovulation
  • Significant undereating or over-exercising, which signals the hypothalamus that the body is not in a safe state for reproduction
  • Elevated prolactin (hyperprolactinemia), which suppresses the HPO axis and disrupts ovulation
  • Perimenopause, during which ovarian response to FSH becomes erratic and cycles lengthen and become unpredictable
  • Coming off hormonal contraception, after which the HPO axis may take several months to resume normal signaling

Because so many different conditions can produce irregular cycles, the pattern of irregularity — combined with other symptoms — helps narrow the field considerably before any testing takes place.

PCOS and Irregular Cycles

Polycystic ovary syndrome is the most common cause of irregular periods in women of reproductive age, affecting an estimated 8 to 13 percent of women globally. Despite its name, the defining feature of PCOS is not cysts on the ovaries but rather a pattern of disrupted ovulation driven by elevated androgens (testosterone and related hormones) and, in most cases, underlying insulin resistance.

In PCOS, the LH surge that normally triggers ovulation fails to fire reliably. Instead, multiple follicles begin developing but none reach full maturity and release. The ovaries may develop the characteristic "string of pearls" appearance on ultrasound — many small, immature follicles arrested in early development — but this finding alone is not diagnostic and can appear in women without PCOS.

Cycles in PCOS are typically long — often 35 to 90 days or more — or absent altogether. Because ovulation is infrequent, progesterone production is low, and the endometrium may build up over an extended period before shedding, sometimes resulting in a very heavy bleed when menstruation does eventually arrive.

Addressing insulin resistance is central to restoring ovulatory function in most women with PCOS. This does not mean a severe low-carbohydrate diet for everyone — it means reducing blood sugar volatility through protein-anchored meals, reducing ultra-processed foods, prioritizing sleep and stress management, and incorporating strength training alongside regular movement.

Thyroid and Stress

Both thyroid dysfunction and chronic stress can disrupt cycle regularity through separate but related mechanisms. Hypothyroidism elevates TRH, which raises prolactin and directly suppresses ovulation. It also impairs the sensitivity of ovarian cells to FSH and LH, making the follicle maturation process sluggish and unreliable. Hyperthyroidism tends to shorten cycles and can cause very light periods or missed periods through a different set of hormonal disturbances.

Stress acts primarily through the hypothalamus. When the stress response is chronically activated, the hypothalamus prioritizes cortisol production over reproductive signaling. GnRH pulses become less frequent and less robust, which reduces FSH and LH output and delays or suppresses ovulation. This is the body's built-in protective mechanism — reproduction is deprioritized when survival demands are high — but in the context of modern chronic stress, it becomes a source of significant cyclical disruption.

Notably, the stress that disrupts ovulation does not have to be dramatic. Ongoing work pressure, insufficient sleep, undereating, and intensive daily exercise can each independently suppress the HPO axis enough to push ovulation later and produce irregular cycles.

How to Start Investigating

If your cycles are consistently irregular, the most productive starting point is systematic observation and targeted testing. Tracking gives you information; testing confirms what the pattern suggests.

  • Track cycle length for at least three months, noting the first day of each period — this establishes your actual pattern rather than your assumed one
  • Use basal body temperature (BBT) charting or LH strips to determine whether and when you are ovulating
  • Request a full thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4, TPO antibodies) if you have any accompanying thyroid symptoms
  • Ask for fasting insulin, fasting glucose, and a testosterone panel if your cycles are consistently long and you have any signs of elevated androgens (acne along the jawline, excess facial or body hair, hair thinning at the crown)
  • Check prolactin if your cycles are absent or very infrequent — elevated prolactin is a treatable and often overlooked cause
  • Consider a cycle day 21 progesterone test to confirm whether ovulation is occurring — a result above 5 ng/mL generally indicates ovulation took place

Irregular cycles are informative, not just inconvenient. They are your body communicating that something in the hormonal chain needs attention — and with the right investigation, the cause is almost always identifiable.
